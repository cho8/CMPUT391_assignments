create table rdf (
	sub text,
	pred text,
	obj text
);


Our strategy for storing the RDF Graph is to use a single table to store the
subject, predicate, and objects, with columns representing each respectively
This way, the schema is relatively simple to store after parsing
For this schema, the object types can be simply
stored as strings without needing to detect the type initially.
When using FILTER in SPARQL, we will need to convert the data type based on
the type it is stored as (based on the predicate). Prefixes will be remapped to
their original URIs in each triple.

The following is an example RDF


@prefix rdf: <http://www.w3.org/1999/02/22-rdf-syntax-ns#> .
@prefix contact: <http://www.w3.org/2000/10/swap/pim/contact#> .
<http://www.w3.org/People/EM/contact#me>
  rdf:type contact:Person;
  contact:fullName "John Doe";
  contact:mailbox <mailto:em@w3.org>;
  contact:personalTitle "Mr.".
<http://www.example.org/index.html> <http://purl.org/dc/elements/1.1/creator> <http://www.example.org/staffid/85740> .
<http://www.example.org/index.html> <http://www.example.org/terms/creation-date> "August 16, 1989" .
<http://www.example.org/index.html> <http://purl.org/dc/elements/1.1/language> "en" .

@prefix exterms: <http://www.example.org/terms/> .
@prefix ex: <http://www.example.org/> .
@prefix dc: <http://purl.org/dc/elements/1.1/> .
@prefix exstaff: <http://www.example.org/staffid/> .
<http://www.example.org/index.html> exterms:creation-date "August 16, 1999" .
ex:index.html   dc:creator              exstaff:85740 .
ex:index.html   exterms:creation-date   "August 16, 1999" .
ex:index.html   dc:language             "en" .
